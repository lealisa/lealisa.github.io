Redesigning the core toolbar in Contour

  • Role

    Design lead on the project

  • Responsibilities

    Research, Strategy, UX, Prototyping, Visual Design & Front-end Support

  • September–November 2017

Contour is one of the principal applications within Palantir’s enterprise platform that allows users to conduct analytical and logical operations on large datasets, and create visual reports to share their findings.

This project focused on improving walk-up usability, feature discovery and overall user experience of the tool by redesigning the apps’s core toolbar. The goal was to empower users to explore Contour’s features on their own and enhance how they interact with their data.

Context & research

Contour is a top-down data analysis application within the Foundry platform1. It allows users of various technical abilities to perform actions on large, unmanageable amounts of complex data—such as appling filters and creating visualizations —to distill that data into meaningful information.

One of Contour’s main value propositions is that it enables users to perform these actions without knowledge of formula syntax, and it creates a visual representation, called a path, of how the data changed from its original source. This feature allows users to easily collaborate with one another, making it a powerful analytical and presentation tool.

Contour’s information architecture, August 2017 (pre-redesign).

While Contour is constantly evolving to solve new user needs, it had reached a point where the application became so unwiedly that users were unable to locate or take advantage of many features. Through conducting internal audits, interviewing product stakeholders, and shadowing users in the field, our team identified Contour’s overall UX as a high priority problem.

Most commonly observed UX issues

The toolbar was hard to navigate and very disorganized. We observed users often not knowing where to find the actions they need. It was also unclear what each action did, or which action was best suited for a task.

While the app allowed users to add actions to the data path in a non-linear way, the insert feature was hidden and unintuitive. Users constantly requested this functionality to be built, even though it already existed.

The app header duplicated some of the primary toolbar functionality in order to accomodate table view-mode and small screen sizes. Users found different ways to add actions jarring and confusing.

In order to understand how users are affected, I decided to created a series of personas to identify the goals of different kinds of users and how they engage with Contour to accomplish those goals.

By identifying difference use cases and isolating indivual problems with each user process, we were able to determine three common pain points that lead to dissatisfaction with Contour.

High-level pain points

  1. Users couldn’t figure out Contour on their own.

    Users required extensive training to understand the tool, and Palantir analysts spent the majority of their time onboarding new users — how might we make the actions available in Contour more approachable and intuitive?

  2. Users didn't take full advantage of the tool’s capabilities.

    Many simple tasks became overly complicated and time consuming because users weren’t aware of what functions were available to them — how might we enable users to find the transform/visualization they need upon landing in Contour?

  3. Users felt overwhelmed by the number of options.

    The toolbar’s lack of hierarchy made the application feel too complex — how might we suggest relevant actions to users?

Initial explorations

After consolidating the research findings and identifying how different user groups are most affected by the UX problems, it was clear that improving the toolbar experience could significantly enhance usability of the application as a whole. I started exploring some low-fidelity design ideas that aimed to solve each pain point we had identified across Contour.

During this process I lead my team through a series of design exercises like “Crazy 8s” which we used to collaboratively identify requirements based on existing workflows and user types. Once we felt confident in our understanding of the user needs and had some loose ideas on how to solve them, I moved into creating a series of low-fidelity wireframes which we used validate and refine our ideas.

One recurring issue with discovery that we aimed to solve was that features were not grouped in a meaningful way. This prevented users from being able to quicly find what they were looking for and we hypothesized that categories would improve action discoverability and help users understand what they do.

After exploring and internally testing different ways of grouping actions—based on usage numbers, action type, alphabetic—I decided to group them based on their functionality.

The final category breakdown. Categories are customizable depending on client needs.

After finalizing the categories, I created a series of mid-fidelity wireframes that outlined how users would find and interact with the groups. To ensure this new grouping was universally accessible, each iteration was tested against our persona use cases.

Examples of some mid-fidelity wireframes.

Once we had narrowed down our mid-fidelity wireframes to two directions that we felt confident in, I created high-fidelity interactive prototypes in Principle that I used to test with users.
