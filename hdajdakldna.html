Redesigning the core toolbar in Contour

  • Role

    Design lead on the project

  • Responsibilities

    Research, Strategy, UX, Prototyping, Visual Design & Front-end Support

  • September–November 2017

Contour is one of the principal applications within Palantir’s enterprise platform that allows users to conduct analytical and logical operations on large datasets, and create visual reports to share their findings.

This project focused on improving walk-up usability, feature discovery and overall user experience of the tool by redesigning the apps’s core toolbar. The goal was to empower users to explore Contour’s features on their own and enhance how they interact with their data.

Context & research

Contour is a top-down data analysis application within the Foundry platform. It allows users of various technical abilities to perform actions on large, unmanageable amounts of complex data—such as appling filters and creating visualizations—to distill that data into meaningful information.

One of Contour’s main value propositions is that it enables users to perform these actions without knowledge of formula syntax, and it creates a visual representation, called a path, of how the data changed from its original source. This feature allows a range of users to easily collaborate with one another, making it a powerful analytical and presentation tool.

Contour’s information architecture, August 2017 (pre-redesign).

While Contour is constantly evolving to solve new user needs, it had reached a point where the application became so complicated that users were unable to locate or take advantage of many features. Through conducting internal audits, interviewing product stakeholders, and shadowing users in the field, our team identified Contour’s overall UX as a high priority problem.

Most commonly observed UX issues

The toolbar was hard to navigate and has become very disorganized. We observed users often not knowing where to find the actions they need. It was also unclear what each action did, or which action was best suited for a task.

While the app allowed users to add actions to the data path in a non-linear way, the insert feature was hidden and unintuitive. Users constantly requested this functionality to be built, even though it already existed.

The app header duplicated some of the primary toolbar functionality in order to accomodate table view-mode and small screen sizes. Users found different ways to add actions jarring and confusing.

After establishing some common painpoints with Contour, we decided to break down our users into groups to understand what specific problems were affecting each type of user. In doing so, we hoped to uncover actionable problems in real user scenarios.

By identifying difference usecases and isolating indivual problems with each user process, I was able to determine three common pain points that lead to dissatisfaction with Contour. To ensure that I was approaching the problem holistically, I wrote a “how might we” statement for each pain point.

High-level pain points

  1. Users couldn’t figure out Contour on their own.

    Users required extensive training to understand the tool, and Palantir analysts spent the majority of their time onboarding new users — how might we make the actions available in Contour more approachable and intuitive?

  2. Users didn't take full advantage of the tool’s capabilities.

    Many simple tasks became overly complicated and time consuming because users weren’t aware of what functions were available to them — how might we enable users to find the transforms/visualizations they need upon landing in Contour?

  3. Users felt overwhelmed by the number of options.

    The toolbar’s lack of hierarchy made the application feel too complex — how might we suggest relevant actions to users?

Initial explorations

After consolidating the research findings and identifying how different user groups are affected by the UX problems, it was clear that improving the toolbar experience could significantly enhance usability of the application as a whole. I started exploring some low-fidelity design ideas that aimed to solve each pain point we had identified across Contour.

During this process I lead my team through a series of design exercises like “Crazy 8s” which we used to collaboratively identify requirements based on existing workflows and user types. Once we felt confident in our understanding of the user needs and had some loose ideas on how to solve them, I moved into creating a series of wireframes which we used to validate and refine our ideas.

One recurring issue with discovery that we aimed to solve was that features were not grouped in a meaningful way. This prevented users from being able to quicly find what they were looking for, and we hypothesized that categories would improve action discoverability and help users understand what they do.

After exploring and internally testing different ways of grouping actions—for example usage numbers, action type, alphabetic—I decided to group the boards into categories based on their functionality. This option proved to be the most intuitive and customizable; it could accommodate an arbitrary number of custom boards and allow users to quickly see all actions with related functionality at once.

The final category breakdown. Categories are customizable depending on client needs.

After finalizing the categories, I created a series of mid-fidelity wireframes that outlined how users would find and interact with the groups. To ensure this new grouping was universally accessible, each iteration was tested against our persona usecases.

Examples of explorations and mid-fidelity wireframes.

After several iterations, we narrowed down our mid-fidelity wireframes to two directions. From this point we felt comfortable moving onto talking with users to test our hypotheses.

Prototyping & testing

To validate our assumptions, I developed two versions (seen above) as prototypes in Principle and tested with real users on-site. Since the two concepts were drastically different, I felt it was important to understand the pros and cons of each one. In Version 1, the Toolbar is replaced with a side column and a drag-and-drop style interaction. Version 2 followed the established pattern with the Toolbar, but included action categories and contextual illustrations.

Verion 1 replaced the horiztonal toolbar with a vertical sidebar that allowed users to drag and drop actions into the path.

During testing sessions, I had multiple users run through various scenarios with each prototype. I recorded quotes, verbal feedback, and notes documenting how easily users were able to accomplish given tasks. These notes helped me understand the pros and cons of each concept and heavily influenced the final design.

The exact quotes are omitted for confidentiality reasons.

From the testing session I learned that Version 1’s sidebar interaction was too different from the existing pattern, and drag-and-drop method of applying new actions was also confusing. However, I also found that users responded positively to the new categories and descriptions from both Versions 1 and 2. While neither prototype provided the solution we were looking for, they both introduced several features that users responded well to. After compiling and evaluating the results of the testing sessions I was able to incorporate the positive improvements from both prototypes into the final design.

Final design

Once the design work for Contour’s new Toolbar had been approved and user-tested, I worked closely with our engineering team to realize our vision for the product.

Initial state

The new toolbar presents users with a more intuitive zero state, having them select from 6 options rather than 12+. Suggested category was also added specifically for the first-time/inexperienced users to make the app feel less intimidating.

Category dropdown

By housing actions within categories, there is ample room for descriptions and contextual illustrations, helping users learn what they are able to do.

Insert board popup

When users hover in-between boards, an “Add New Board” indicator appears making it easy for users to search for and add actions anywhere in their path.

Search

The newly added search functionality not only helps new users discover actions based on names and dscriptions, but also makes it much easier for power-users to quickly add specifc actions.

Consistency across different modes and viewport sizes

The new toolbar is responsive and adapts to each users screen size. This flexibility allows users to take full advantage of Contour’s capabilities at any desktop size.

Bonus: Illustrations for all action types

Along with writting descriptions for each action, I also created an abstracted visualization of each board to provide users with context and a concise understanding of each action.

Evaluation

After shipping the new toolbar, our team kept a close eye on incoming metrics and followed up with users and analysts in the field to see how it was being received. We found that our UX improvements had a number of positive impacts including reducing required training sessions, an increase in the types of boards being used, and a huge increase in the number of board inserts, implying that users now had a much better understanding of how to use Contour.

This graph demonstrates a dramatic upswing in the Insert and Search functionalities after launch and also indicates an incredibly positive response to board Categories.

Not only was there an overall increase in the amount of boards being used, but this data also strongly indicates that users are interested in applying a larger variety of actions.

Redesigning Contour’s toolbar was a challenging effort due to the sheer number of user variables and workflows we needed to accommodate. At the end of the day I believe we found success by keeping an open mind and collaborating with users throughout the process. By working closely with our clients and continuously iterating on our hypotheses, we were able to develop a solution that was practical, efficient, and universally accessible.
